Shopping with Obsidian, Dataview, and Obsidian Web Clipper

I used to be very into skincare. Like so many things, that tapered out during covid, when I got depressed and stopped existing for a good while.

Now I'm slowly getting back into it, but as you might expect, a couple of years of being stored in the back of the bathroom cupboard did not do my skincare products much good. That caffeine serum is not supposed to be coffee brown, and so a great purge took place.

That means I need replacements, and while I could re-buy the same stuff, well…I'm a nerd with a boring project on my schedule, so it's time to commit at least seven times as much effort than strictly necessary.

Grabbing potential purchases with Obsidian Web Clipper

My main tool for anything text-based is Obsidian, and with the help of Obsidian Web Clipper I can easily grab stuff on the web. For keeping track of potential purchases, I use the Product template from the official clipper templates repository, with a few modifications to fit my vault's data structure.

So my first step is to simply go to my online store of choice, look through the relevant category, clip anything that looks interesting, and add the #skincare tag to it. This means that instead of having dozens of browser tabs open, I have one markdown note for each product, and those notes contain useful metadata for helping me choose.

Adding price and size information

Well, mostly. I would really like to know the price per unit of measurement as well. If you've ever seen the prices on skincare, you'll understand why – I just threw away a small fortune, I want to get my money's worth in the replacements!

The generic Product template doesn't capture things like size at all, and fails to get the price on my preferred store. I could make a custom clipper template for this specific store, but it doesn't quite feel worth it yet.

It's easy enough to simply add this data manually after clipping, so I add the keys price and size to the frontmatter, and fill them with values like kr 115 and 30 ml as I go. In the end, each note has frontmatter like this:

---
url: http://example.com/product
tags: [skincare]
price: kr 225
size: 30 ml
---

The Dataview plugin makes the actual calculation simple. With a code block like this in a separate note, I get a neat table of products with everything calculated for me.

```dataview
TABLE

price, 
size, 
default(number(price), 1)/default(number(size), 1) AS "Price per unit"

FROM #skincare

WHERE icontains(url, "example.com")
```

I could have stopped there, grabbed the option that's cheapest per unit, and called it a day, but…

…what if I start comparing ingredients while I'm at it?

Adding ingredients

My clipper template doesn't grab ingredients either, but once again it's easy enough to add manually. Ingredient lists are usually comma separated, and so are the lists in Obsidian's frontmatter. I can copy the ingredients off the product page, paste them into the frontmatter, and surround the list with square brackets:

---
url: http://example.com/product
tags: [skincare]
price: kr 225
size: 30 ml
ingredients: [Petrolatum, Lanolin, Paraffinum Liquidum, Cera Alba, Ricinus Communis Seed Oil, Tocopherol]
---

Great!

The next question is how do I do something useful with this? I know a few ingredients and what they do, but I'm by no means an expert.

Collecting ingredient information

There is, however, expert explanations readily available on the internet in the form of Paula's Choice Skin Care Ingredient Dictionary. Off I go, looking up individual ingredients, and clipping the ones that are useful to me.

While the information is top notch, the code for the individual pages is messy, which means the clipped notes come out messy, too. After manually cleaning up several notes, I took the time to make a web clipper template for the skin care ingredient dictionary. The clipped notes are still not perfect, but now require much less work.

Connecting the notes

The next step is to connect the ingredient notes with the product notes. At the bottom of each ingredient note, I add the following code block:

```dataview
TABLE

price, 
size, 
default(number(price), 1)/default(number(size), 1) AS "Price per unit"

FROM #skincare 

WHERE icontains(ingredients, this.file.name)
```

Now I can see which of my clipped products contain this ingredient.

It would also be useful to see what interesting ingredients a given product contains, so I add a similar code block to each product note:

```dataview
TABLE

rating

FROM #hudpleie OR #skincare 

WHERE icontains(this.ingredients, file.name)

SORT length(rating) DESC, file.name ASC
```

This gives me a neat little table of the ingredients I care about, along with their rating, sorted from best to worst.

In theory, that's it. I can now go shopping and make well-informed decisions…

Going the extra mile

…but since I now have such a useful system, why not make the most of it?

Going back to the store, I clip all the products I already have, adding the ingredients as above. Now I can clearly see which key ingredients are already covered in my routine, and I can trim down my list of potential products to a more focused selection.

I can also discover the products in my collection that can't live up to their hype, because there just isn't enough of a given ingredient, or none of the ingredients do what the description claims!

Conclusion

In what could easily have become a shopping bonanza, I ended up with just five products. Just two(!) of them are replacements for the stuff I threw out, since it turned out everything else I want is already covered! The rest is all refills of staples like sunscreen and my daily moisturiser.

In addition I now have detailed notes on all my products, and I've been able to confidently mark the ones I won't be replacing when they run out. If a new product catches my eye, I can compare it to what I already have, and make educated assumptions about how useful it would be.

All in all, I should have done this a long time ago. I could have saved so much money!
